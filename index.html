Mental Health and Mental Illness on University Campuses.

Mental health issues are a significant barrier to academic success and happiness among college students. While help is available, there is an unnecessary shame surrounding these issues and on top of this, services have proven to be inadequate. As a result, mental health is not discussed enough and a large portion of students suffer alone. In order to provide sufficient services to their sudents, universities must focus on developing accessible and thorough resources, while working to eradicate the stigma of mental illness.

This website will explore methods to provide pro-active campus-wide support and to eradicate the stigma surrounding mental illness.

The Facts and Why We Need To Talk About Mental Health

• Nearly one third of all college students report having felt so depressed that they had trouble functioning

• 26% of Americans ages 18 and older live with a diagnosable mental health disorder in a given year

• More than 80% of college students felt overwhelmed in the past year and 45% have felt things were hopeless

• Compared to older adults, the 18-24 year old age group shows the lowest rate of helpseeking

• Suicide is the 2nd leading cause of death among college students, claiming the lives of 1,100 students each year

• 80-90% of college students who die by suicide were not seeking help from their college counseling centers

Where University Mental Health Programs Fall Short:

1. Inadequate Resources

Unfortunately, most counseling centers work with restricted resources. In the US, the average university has one educated counselor for every 1800 students which is fewer than 1 counselor for every 1,000 to 1,500 students recommended by the International Association of Counseling Services.

As colleges are unable to grow at the same rate as students seeking help, some students are slipping through the cracks due to long waits for treatment and a lasting stigma associated with mental health issues. Even if students ask for and receive help, not all cases can be treated on campus. As a result, many professional off-campus centers are stepping in, but at the expense of the student and his/her/their family. These professional program can amount to more than $10,000 per year and are many times not covered by health insurance. However, especially in rural areas, where options for off-campus care are limited, universities are feeling pressure to do more.

The NAMI 2016 research discovered that over the last 10 years universities have increased resources devoted to rapid-access services in response to rising demand from students — including walk-in appointments and crisis treatment for students demonstrating signs of distress. But long-term treatment services, including recurring appointments and specialized counseling, decreased on average during that time period. However, there is still a great need for more.

2. The Stigma

People with mental illness are a part of one of the most stigmatized groups in American culture. Many of the over 45 million people in the US who suffer from some type of mental health disorder may describe and define stigma using one of these words or phrases: hate, discrimination, prejudice, fear inducing, humiliating, hurtful.

Stigma is directly tied to discrimination and hate. Often, those with mental illness are portrayed as violent, incompetent and disdainful. Perpetuation of hurtful and inaccurate stereotypes by the media is especially harmful because of the profound role it has in shaping and influencing social mores and attitudes. Whether consciously or not, the community as a whole ingests these negative attitudes, which they then use to stigmatize those with mental disorders.As a result, people struggling with mental health challenges may not get the help they need for fear they’ll be discriminated against.

Mental illness stigma has been identified by national policy makers as an important barrier to help seeking for mental health. According to a study by the National Alliance on Mental Illness, stigma remains the number one barrier to students seeking help. As a result of public stigma, personal stigma arrises which is when people with mental illness internalize the negative perceptions and stereotypes. This can result in even more severe mental illness.

Changes To Be Made

An increasing number of students with mental health conditions attend colleges across the country, and colleges need to take these issues seriously.

Educate Faculty

Professors/RA's should know about warning signs, symptoms, and prevalence of mental health issues. They must understand that mental health conditions, even those that are invisible, are real and how hard it can be for students living with these conditions.

They should be proactive in connecting students to help and encouragng them to seek help. Faculty should address mental health in the classroom. They should understand that students living with mental health conditions can be succesful even if they need to complete the work in different ways.

Educate Students/Peers

Students should be aware that having mental illness is more common that they think and they are not alone. Peer support is extremely important and they should try to be emphatetic, aware, and patient. Students can and should get regular mental health checkups just like physical checkups.

Students should know the signs and symptoms of mental health conditions. Be conscious of changes in sleep patters, eating habits, behaviors, perceptions, and social interactions. Mental health conditions are treatable and help is available. It is okay to seek help.

Reform University Mental Health Programs

• Start a mental health and wellness course for students and professors.

• Increase visibility, publicity, and promotion of mental health issues and resources.

• Create an informative and easily accessible website.

• 24/7 access to help. Make scheduling appointments online easy.

• Allocate funding to student groups that work to fight stigma, such as activeminds.

• Communicate with professors to more easily give students with mental illness accomodations.
